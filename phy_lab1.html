Ohm's Law

Ohm’s law is one of the most popular and important laws that helps us define the relationship between voltage, current and resistance. The law was first established by a German physicist named Georg Simon Ohm, and was the most important theory to describe the quantitative characteristics of the physics of electricity.

What Is Ohm’s Law?

Ohm’s law states that the current through a conductor between two points is directly proportional to the voltage across the two points. Ohm’s law formula is written as;

V ∝ I

Therefore, V = RI where R is a constant called resistance. R depends on the dimensions of the conductor and also on the material of the conductor. Its SI unit is Ohm (Ω).

Advantages:

The advatages are stated as folllows:

  • Predictability: Ohm's Law provides a simple and straightforward mathematical relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in an electrical circuit
  • Universal Applicability: Ohm's Law is applicable to a wide range of electrical circuits, from simple resistive circuits to more complex ones involving reactive components like capacitors and inductors.
  • Ease of Use: The mathematical formulation of Ohm's Law (V = IR) is relatively simple and easy to understand, making it accessible to students and practitioners at all levels of expertise.
  • Consistency: Ohm's Law holds true under a wide variety of conditions, including changes in temperature, pressure, and other environmental factors.

Disadvantages:

The disadvatages are stated as folllows:

  • Assumes Linear Behavior: Ohm's Law is based on the assumption of linearity, meaning that the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance remains constant over a wide range of values.
  • Resistance Variation: Ohm's Law assumes that resistance is constant and does not vary with factors such as temperature or frequency
  • Complex Circuits: While Ohm's Law is useful for analyzing simple circuits, it may become less practical for complex circuits involving multiple components and interactions.

Applications of Ohm’s Law

Ohm’s law is very useful and has several applications. A few are listed below:

  • It is widely used in circuit analysis.
  • It is used in ammeter, multimeter, etc.
  • It is used to design resistors.
  • It is used to get the desired circuit drop in circuit design.
  • Advanced laws such as Kirchhoff’s Norton’s law, Thevenin’s law are based on Ohm’s law.
  • Electric heaters, kettles and other types of equipment working principle follow Ohm’s law.
  • A laptop and mobile charger using DC power supply in operation and working principle of DC power supply depend on Ohm’s law.
